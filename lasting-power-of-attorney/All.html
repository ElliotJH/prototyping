1. What is a lasting power of attorney?

A lasting power of attorney (LPA) is a powerful legal document that allows someone (the “donor”) to choose other people (the “attorneys”) to make decisions on their behalf if they lose the mental capacity to make those decisions themselves. If you're not yet familiar with the process we suggest that you find out more before continuing.

3. Who is the LPA for?

This person the LPA is for is known as the 'donor'. The donor appoints one or more people ("attorneys") to make decisions on their behalf when they are no longer able, or lack the mental capacity, to make decisions themselves. Learn more…

5. Who are the attorneys?

An attorney is a person appointed by the donor to make all decisions on their behalf when they are unable to make decisions for themselves. An attorney should be a person who knows the donor well and who they trust to understand their wishes and to make decisions in their best interests. Attorneys must be aged 18 or over. You can appoint more than one attorney. Learn more…

9. Will the donor notify anyone when the LPA is registered?

The donor can choose to formally notify people they know well (eg. family or close friends) when the LPA is registered. This is an important safeguard as it means people who care about the donor's best interests can double-check that all is well and object if they are concerned about anything. Learn more…

10. Is the donor doing this of their own free will?

You must name at least one independent person (called a "certificate provider") who can confirm that the donor understands the significance of the LPA, that they have not been put under pressure to make it and there has been no fraud involved. Not everyone is allowed to act in this capacity. Learn more…
